Campaign Element 1 · 25%

Social Media Copywriter

Developing platform-specific copy to recruit student innovators and drive applications for Red Bull Basement University.

As social media copywriter for this campaign, my role was to translate the Red Bull Basement brief into compelling, audience-targeted written content across multiple platforms. The campaign objective was clear: inspire university students to apply for the Red Bull Basement programme by communicating its value proposition — global competition, mentorship, and the opportunity to turn ideas into real-world impact. According to Hackley and Hackley (2021), effective advertising copy must balance emotional resonance with a clear call to action, which guided my approach throughout.

My copy strategy was rooted in understanding the target audience — digitally native students aged 18–25 who respond to authentic, energetic brand voices. I developed three distinct copy formats: awareness posts to introduce the programme, engagement posts to stimulate interaction, and conversion posts driving direct applications. This mirrors the AIDA framework (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action), which remains a cornerstone of direct response copywriting (Fill & Turnbull, 2019).

📱 Instagram Caption — Awareness

Draft Copy #1

"You've got the idea. Red Bull Basement gives you the world. 🌍 Apply now and compete globally — from your university to the international stage. What problem are you solving? #RedBullBasement #UniversityInnovation #StudentEntrepreneur"

💼 LinkedIn — Conversion

If you're a student with a vision for technology or social impact, this is your moment. Applications now open. #RedBullBasement #StudentInnovation #Entrepreneurship"

Platform: LinkedIn · Tone: Professional, informative · Audience: Students + staff

Theory Application

Tone of voice consistency across platforms is essential in building brand equity (Kapferer, 2012). While each copy piece was adapted to platform norms — informal and emoji-driven on Instagram, concise on X, professional on LinkedIn — the brand voice remained recognisably Red Bull: bold, confident, and action-oriented. This approach aligns with integrated marketing communications (IMC) theory (Kliatchko, 2008), ensuring message coherence across channels.

Writing copy for this campaign strengthened my understanding of platform-specific communication. The key challenge was maintaining brand authenticity while adapting tone and length across three distinct channels. Feedback from my team confirmed that the LinkedIn copy was particularly effective in its professional framing, while the Instagram caption generated the most engagement in our internal review. This experience reinforced the value of audience segmentation in copywriting strategy (De Pelsmacker et al., 2018).

Campaign Element 2 · 25%

Graphic Designer

Creating on-brand visual assets to support the Red Bull Basement digital campaign across social media channels.

In my role as graphic designer, I was responsible for producing the visual assets that would accompany the campaign copy across social media. Visual communication is fundamental to digital marketing effectiveness — according to Lidwell et al. (2010), audiences process images 60,000 times faster than text, making strong visual design critical to campaign cut-through. My design brief was to create materials that felt authentically Red Bull while communicating the energy and opportunity of the Basement programme.

I used Canva to produce my primary asset (shown below), drawing on Red Bull's established brand guidelines — the signature red and navy palette, bold typography, and energetic visual language. The design incorporated the three core programme pillars: idea submission, mentorship, and global competition, structured to deliver information hierarchy clearly and quickly. This reflects Gestalt design principles (proximity, hierarchy, contrast), which are essential tools in visual communication design (Landa, 2019).

Design Rationale

The colour strategy mirrors Red Bull's brand identity: red for energy and urgency, dark tones for credibility and depth. Bold sans-serif typography reinforces the brand's action-oriented personality. The three-column layout was chosen to communicate the programme journey at a glance — a structure informed by the F-pattern reading model, which shows users scan content left-to-right across the top before moving down (Nielsen, 2006). Each column uses a short headline and brief descriptor, minimising cognitive load while maximising information retention.

In addition to the primary graphic, I reviewed Red Bull's existing visual campaign materials (panel event imagery, past Basement event photography) to ensure tonal consistency. The panel discussion image below informed my understanding of how Red Bull presents authority and credibility in its Basement events — which I reflected in the professional, structured layout of my design.

Brand Energy ReferenceRed Bull event photography — used to understand the brand's high-energy visual language and scale

This role developed my practical skills in brand-consistent design and visual storytelling. The most significant learning was understanding how to balance creativity with brand constraint — designing something original that still felt unmistakably Red Bull. Going forward, I would develop more A/B design variants to test visual performance, as recommended by content optimisation frameworks (Chaffey & Ellis-Chadwick, 2022).

Campaign Element 3 · 25%

Content Scheduler

Planning and scheduling a structured content calendar to maximise reach and engagement throughout the campaign.

As content scheduler, I was responsible for planning the publication timeline for all campaign assets across our chosen platforms: Instagram, X (Twitter), LinkedIn, and TikTok. Effective content scheduling is not simply about timing posts — it requires strategic understanding of audience behaviour, platform algorithms, and campaign phase objectives. Chaffey and Ellis-Chadwick (2022) note that content cadence is one of the most overlooked drivers of organic social media performance.

I structured the campaign across three phases aligned to the Red Bull Basement application lifecycle: Awareness (Weeks 1–2), Consideration (Weeks 3–4), and Conversion (Week 5). This phased approach mirrors the marketing funnel and ensures that content serves the right audience at the right stage of the decision journey (Kotler et al., 2019). Scheduling Strategy

Platform posting times were informed by audience analytics data and industry benchmarks. Instagram posts were scheduled for Monday and Thursday evenings (7–9pm) when student engagement peaks; LinkedIn content was timed to Tuesday mornings to align with professional browsing patterns. This approach reflects Zarella's (2013) research on social media timing, which demonstrates statistically significant engagement differences based on post time. The three-phase funnel structure mirrors the Awareness–Consideration–Conversion model established in digital marketing theory (Chaffey & Ellis-Chadwick, 2022).

Managing the content calendar required close collaboration with the copywriter and designer roles within the team — coordinating asset delivery deadlines against publication dates. This reinforced my understanding of campaign project management and the interdependency of production roles. Tools used included a shared spreadsheet for scheduling and a group messaging channel for sign-off coordination. Going forward I would incorporate a dedicated social media management tool such as Hootsuite or Buffer to automate scheduling and track performance metrics in real time (Tuten & Solomon, 2017).

Personal Reflection · 25%

Gibbs Reflective Cycle

A structured reflection on my contributions, learning, and professional development throughout the Red Bull Basement campaign.

To structure my reflection, I have applied Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988), a widely used framework in professional practice that moves through six stages: Description, Feelings, Evaluation, Analysis, Conclusion, and Action Plan. This model encourages not just a description of events but a deeper examination of what was learned and what should change — making it particularly valuable for a live client project such as this.

Stage 1

Description

I contributed to the Red Bull Basement digital campaign in three roles: social media copywriter, graphic designer, and content scheduler. Working as part of a student team, we developed campaign materials aimed at recruiting university students to apply for the Basement programme. My responsibilities spanned creative writing, visual design in Canva, and managing a five-week content calendar across four platforms.

Stage 2

Feelings

Initially, I felt both excited and anxious — excited by the real-world brief, and anxious about producing work that met Red Bull's brand standards. As the project progressed, my confidence grew, particularly after receiving positive feedback on my Instagram copy from teammates. I found the graphic design role the most challenging, as I was less confident in visual tools, but completing the asset gave me a genuine sense of accomplishment.

Stage 3

Evaluation

Overall, the campaign was a positive experience. My copy was well-received and the content schedule was delivered on time. However, I underestimated the time required for the graphic design element, which created pressure in Week 2. Team communication was generally strong, though there were moments where clearer role boundaries would have prevented overlap and duplication of effort.

Stage 4

Analysis

Reflecting on the challenges, my time management issue in the design phase stemmed from a lack of experience with Canva's more advanced features. According to Kolb (1984), experiential learning requires active experimentation — I should have trialled the tool earlier in the process. The team communication issue reflects Tuckman's (1965) "storming" stage of group development, which is a normal but manageable phase that benefits from agreed norms and clear task ownership.

Stage 5

Conclusion

This project taught me that effective campaign delivery requires both creative skill and project management discipline. I have a stronger foundation in social media copywriting than I realised, and a genuine interest in content strategy. My design skills need development, and my instinct to take on multiple roles — while demonstrating initiative — occasionally stretched my capacity. Future projects will benefit from more defined personal timelines alongside the team schedule.

Stage 6

Action Plan

See below — I have developed a structured action plan with specific, time-bound development goals informed by this reflection and by professional development frameworks (Cottrell, 2015).
